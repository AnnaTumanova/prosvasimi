# Disability Employment in Europe vs USA: What Employers Need to Know

## Introduction

Disability inclusion is no longer only a social responsibility topic. It is becoming a business, legal, and talent strategy priority.

Across Europe and the United States, employers are facing labour shortages, skills gaps, ageing populations, and growing expectations around diversity, equity, inclusion, and accessibility. At the same time, millions of people with disabilities remain excluded from the labour market — not because they lack skills, but because hiring systems, workplaces, technologies, and employer attitudes are often not accessible.

For companies, this creates both a challenge and an opportunity.

Employers who understand disability inclusion can access a wider talent pool, improve workplace culture, strengthen brand reputation, and build more resilient teams. But to do this well, they need to understand the differences between Europe and the USA — especially around employment rates, legal frameworks, recruitment practices, and workplace accommodations.

## 1. The employment gap is still significant

In the European Union, the disability employment gap remains high. According to Eurostat, in 2024 the employment gap between people with and without disabilities in the EU was **24.0 percentage points**. This means people with disabilities are still much less likely to be employed than people without disabilities.

In the United States, the gap is also clear. The U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics reported that in 2025, **22.8% of people with a disability were employed**, compared with **65.2% of people without a disability**. The unemployment rate for people with disabilities was **8.3%**, about twice the rate for people without disabilities.

For employers, these numbers show that disability inclusion is not a niche issue. It is a major labour-market issue.

The key question is not: "Are there enough qualified disabled candidates?"

The better question is: "Are our hiring processes, job descriptions, tools, interviews, offices, and management practices accessible enough for qualified disabled candidates to succeed?"

## 2. Europe and the USA use different systems, but the goal is similar

Europe and the USA approach disability employment through different legal and cultural frameworks.

In Europe, disability employment is often discussed through the language of:

- **Equal treatment**
- **Reasonable accommodation**
- **Social inclusion**
- **Employment participation**
- **Supported employment**
- **Accessibility**
- **Anti-discrimination law**
- **Public employment support**
- **Quota systems in some countries**

In the USA, the conversation often focuses on:

- **ADA compliance**
- **Reasonable accommodation**
- **Equal opportunity employment**
- **Vocational rehabilitation**
- **Workplace adjustments**
- **Disability disclosure**
- **Inclusive hiring**
- **Remote work and flexible work**

The legal systems are different, but the employer responsibility is similar: companies must create fair conditions so that people with disabilities can apply, work, grow, and contribute.

## 3. Reasonable accommodation is central in both markets

A major concept in both Europe and the USA is **reasonable accommodation**.

In the EU, employers must provide reasonable accommodation to employees with disabilities, as long as the changes do not create a disproportionate burden for the business. Accommodations can include changes to the workplace, equipment, working patterns, or task organisation.

In the USA, under the Americans with Disabilities Act, employers must provide reasonable accommodation to qualified employees and applicants with disabilities, unless doing so would cause undue hardship. Reasonable accommodation applies not only to the job itself, but also to the application process and equal access to employment benefits.

For employers, this means accommodation should not begin after hiring. It should start at recruitment.

Examples include:

| Area          | Accommodation examples                                                            |
| ------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Recruitment   | Accessible application forms, alternative interview formats, extra time for tests |
| Workplace     | Ergonomic equipment, accessible office layout, assistive technology               |
| Working time  | Flexible hours, part-time options, medical appointment flexibility                |
| Work location | Remote or hybrid work where possible                                              |
| Communication | Written instructions, captioned meetings, clear task documentation                |
| Management    | Regular check-ins, adjusted performance processes, structured onboarding          |

The best employers treat accommodation as a normal part of workforce management, not as an exception.

## 4. Remote and flexible work are accessibility tools

Remote work has changed the disability employment conversation.

For some people with disabilities, remote or hybrid work can reduce barriers related to transport, fatigue, chronic illness, sensory overload, mobility limitations, or inaccessible office spaces. This does not mean all disabled employees want remote work, but it does mean flexibility can be a powerful accessibility tool.

Employers should avoid treating remote work only as a benefit. In many cases, it can be part of reasonable accommodation.

This is especially important for digital roles, customer support, data work, administration, marketing, HR, finance, operations, and many knowledge-based jobs. Companies that remove unnecessary office requirements may reach strong candidates who would otherwise be excluded.

However, remote work must also be accessible. That means:

- Using accessible software
- Captioning meetings when needed
- Documenting decisions in writing
- Avoiding informal exclusion from team communication
- Making promotion opportunities available to remote employees
- Training managers to lead distributed teams inclusively

Flexibility without inclusion can still create isolation. The goal is not only to allow people to work remotely, but to make remote workers fully included in the organisation.

## 5. Europe has strong policy language, but employers need practical tools

Europe has many policy frameworks supporting disability employment, including the European Disability Employment Package, which aims to support Member States in improving social inclusion and economic autonomy for people with disabilities.

However, many employers still struggle with practical implementation.

They may support disability inclusion in principle, but not know how to:

- Write accessible job descriptions
- Adapt interviews
- Discuss accommodation appropriately
- Choose accessible HR software
- Support employees with invisible disabilities
- Train managers
- Measure inclusion outcomes
- Avoid tokenism
- Build partnerships with disability organisations

This creates a gap between policy and practice.

For European employers, the next step is to move from "we are open to everyone" to "our hiring process is actually accessible to everyone."

## 6. The USA has clearer employer-facing compliance language

In the USA, the ADA has created a strong employer-facing language around accommodation, non-discrimination, and equal opportunity. Many American employers are familiar with terms such as ADA compliance, reasonable accommodation, undue hardship, and interactive process.

This can make the topic more practical for HR teams.

However, compliance alone is not enough. A company can technically comply with the law and still fail to build a truly inclusive workplace.

For example, an employer may provide accommodations but still have:

- Biased interview evaluation
- Inaccessible internal software
- Managers uncomfortable discussing disability
- Limited career progression for disabled employees
- A culture where people fear disclosure
- Few disabled employees in leadership roles

The lesson for employers in both Europe and the USA is clear: legal compliance is the minimum. Inclusion requires culture, systems, and accountability.

## 7. What employers should change first

Disability inclusion can feel complex, but employers do not need to solve everything at once. The best approach is to start with the points where candidates and employees experience the biggest barriers.

### Step 1: Review job descriptions

Many job descriptions exclude candidates unintentionally.

Avoid unnecessary requirements such as:

- "must be able to work under constant pressure"
- "must have excellent verbal communication" when written communication is enough
- "must be able to lift" unless it is truly essential
- "full-time office presence required" when remote work is possible
- vague phrases like "high energy," "fast-paced," or "rockstar"

Instead, focus on essential skills, outcomes, and flexibility.

### Step 2: Make applications accessible

Online application systems should work with screen readers, keyboard navigation, accessible forms, and clear instructions. Employers should also offer an alternative application method when needed.

### Step 3: Offer interview adjustments

Candidates should be able to request reasonable adjustments without fear of discrimination. This can include extra time, written questions, remote interviews, accessible locations, breaks, or alternative assessment formats.

### Step 4: Train hiring managers

Many barriers come from discomfort or lack of knowledge. Hiring managers need simple, practical training on disability inclusion, legal boundaries, respectful communication, and fair evaluation.

### Step 5: Build accommodation processes

Employees should know how to request accommodations, who handles the process, how privacy is protected, and what timelines to expect.

### Step 6: Measure outcomes

Employers should track whether disabled candidates are applying, progressing through recruitment stages, being hired, staying, and being promoted. Without measurement, inclusion remains only a statement.

## 8. What employers should not do

Many companies make mistakes even when they have good intentions.

Employers should avoid:

- Treating disability inclusion as charity
- Hiring people only for low-skilled or symbolic roles
- Asking inappropriate medical questions during recruitment
- Assuming what a person can or cannot do
- Making accommodation processes complicated or slow
- Requiring office presence without a clear business reason
- Using inaccessible tests or platforms
- Placing all responsibility on the disabled employee
- Communicating inclusion externally without changing internal practices

Disability inclusion should be based on skills, dignity, accessibility, and equal opportunity — not pity or public relations.

## 9. Why inclusive hiring benefits employers

Hiring people with disabilities is not only the right thing to do. It is also a smart business decision.

Inclusive employers can benefit from:

- Access to a larger talent pool
- Stronger employee loyalty
- Better problem-solving and innovation
- Improved employer reputation
- Higher accessibility standards for all employees
- Better customer understanding
- Stronger compliance and risk management
- More resilient workplace culture

Accessibility improvements often help everyone. Clear communication, flexible work, structured onboarding, accessible documents, and better management practices benefit disabled and non-disabled employees alike.

## Conclusion

Disability employment is one of the most important inclusion challenges in today's labour market.

In Europe, the disability employment gap remains significant, despite strong policy frameworks and growing attention to accessibility. In the USA, the ADA gives employers a clearer compliance structure, but disabled workers still face major barriers to employment and career growth.

For employers, the lesson is simple:

Do not wait for perfect conditions. Start by making hiring accessible. Train managers. Offer reasonable accommodations. Build flexible work models. Measure outcomes. Listen to disabled employees and candidates.

Disability inclusion is not a favour to a small group of people. It is a necessary part of building a fair, modern, and competitive workplace.

**The companies that understand this now will be better prepared for the future of work.**
